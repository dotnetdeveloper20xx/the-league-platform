# Facilities and Equipment

These two modules handle the physical assets that clubs manage — the grounds, pitches, courts, and clubhouses on one side, and the bats, balls, machines, and medical kits on the other. They are separate modules because their lifecycles and business rules are quite different, but I am covering them together because they share a common theme of physical asset management and because understanding them together gives you a complete picture of how clubs manage their tangible resources.

Let me start with Facilities. A Facility entity represents a bookable physical space that the club manages. It has a name like "Main Cricket Ground" or "Astroturf Pitch 1" or "Indoor Nets," a type (Field, Studio, Gym, ClubHouse, ChangingRoom, MeetingRoom, Pool), a description that provides details about the space, and a capacity representing how many people can use it simultaneously. Facilities are tenant-scoped — each club manages their own facilities independently.

The primary business logic in the Facilities module revolves around booking and conflict detection. When someone wants to book a facility for a specific time slot — say the main pitch from 2pm to 5pm on Saturday — the system needs to verify that no other booking overlaps with that time. This is a classic interval overlap problem in computer science. Two bookings conflict if one starts before the other ends AND ends after the other starts. The mathematical condition is: booking A conflicts with booking B if A.StartTime is less than B.EndTime AND A.EndTime is greater than B.StartTime. Any booking request must be checked against all existing confirmed bookings for the same facility on the same day.

The conflict detection must be atomic — if two people try to book the same slot simultaneously, only one should succeed. This is handled through database-level constraints or optimistic concurrency. The booking handler queries for conflicting bookings within a transaction, and if none are found, creates the new booking. If a conflict is detected between the query and the insert (a race condition), the transaction fails and the user is told the slot is no longer available.

Facilities can have different pricing for peak and off-peak hours. A cricket ground might be free for club members during weekday mornings (off-peak) but have a hire fee of fifty pounds for external bookings on weekend match days (peak). The pricing model associates time ranges with price tiers — weekday evenings might be one rate, weekends another, and bank holidays a premium rate. This allows clubs to maximize revenue from their facilities while keeping them accessible to members during quieter times.

Maintenance scheduling adds another dimension to facility management. If a pitch is being re-laid, a gym is being refurbished, or a clubhouse is being deep-cleaned, the facility needs to be marked as unavailable for a period. Maintenance windows block out time slots just like bookings do — no one can book a facility that is under maintenance. The system can track scheduled maintenance (planned in advance) and emergency maintenance (facility damaged and needs immediate repair). Maintenance records include the type of work, the contractor or staff responsible, the estimated completion date, and the actual completion date.

The seed data creates sixteen facilities across four clubs with realistic configurations. Teddington Cricket Club has a main ground (capacity two hundred), indoor nets (capacity sixteen), a clubhouse (capacity one hundred and twenty), and outdoor practice nets (capacity twenty-four). Highbury United has a main pitch (capacity three hundred), training pitch (capacity fifty), gym (capacity thirty), and changing rooms (capacity forty). Richmond Hockey Club has two astroturf pitches, a clubhouse with viewing balcony, and a meeting room. The MCC has Lord's main ground (capacity thirty thousand — yes, really), the nursery ground, the indoor school with twelve lanes, and the historic Long Room. These varied capacities and types give you realistic data for building booking calendars, availability views, and facility management interfaces.

Now let me shift to Equipment. The Equipment module tracks physical items that clubs own, lend out, maintain, and eventually replace. An EquipmentItem entity has a name, a category (Sports, Training, Electronics, Medical, Audio, Tools), a condition rating (New, Excellent, Good, Fair, Poor), a location describing where the item is normally stored, a purchase date, a purchase value, and an annual depreciation rate as a percentage.

The depreciation tracking is more than just nice-to-have — it is genuinely useful for clubs that need to report asset values for insurance purposes or for annual accounts. If a bowling machine was purchased two years ago for two thousand eight hundred pounds with a fifteen percent annual depreciation rate, its current book value is approximately two thousand and twenty-three pounds. The system can calculate current values for all equipment items, giving the club treasurer an accurate picture of total asset value at any point in time.

Equipment loans track which items are currently out with members or coaches. A coach might have a set of training cones on semi-permanent loan. A member might borrow a bowling machine for a weekend practice session. The loan record captures who has the item, when they took it, when it is due back, and whether it has been returned. Overdue loans generate reminders — if a member has had the video camera for two weeks past the due date, the system sends them a nudge.

Equipment reservations are forward-looking — they reserve an item for a specific future date or session. The youth coach might reserve the bowling machine for every Saturday morning academy session. A club manager might reserve the PA system for the awards dinner. Reservations prevent double-booking of equipment, similar to how facility bookings prevent double-booking of spaces.

Maintenance tracking for equipment records service history. A bowling machine needs annual servicing. A defibrillator needs monthly battery checks. The heavy roller needs seasonal maintenance. Each maintenance record captures what was done, who did it, the cost, and when the next service is due. The system can flag items that are overdue for maintenance, helping clubs stay on top of safety-critical equipment like defibrillators and first aid kits.

Condition tracking changes over time. When an item is purchased, it starts as New. After a year of use, it might be downgraded to Excellent, then Good, then Fair. When it reaches Poor condition, the club knows it needs replacement. Condition changes are recorded with timestamps, creating a history of the item's deterioration. This helps with budgeting — if training cones typically last two years before needing replacement, the club can plan their equipment budget accordingly.

The seed data creates nineteen equipment items across four clubs with realistic values and categories. Items range from eighty-five pounds for a set of training cones to fifteen thousand pounds for a full set of cricket covers. Depreciation rates range from zero (medical items that do not depreciate for accounting purposes) to twenty-five percent (electronics that lose value quickly). Each item has a condition rating and a storage location. This gives you data for building inventory management screens, loan tracking interfaces, maintenance schedules, and asset valuation reports.

Both modules follow the same architectural pattern as everything else in the system — domain entities with business rules enforced through methods, a DbContext with its own schema (facilities and equipment respectively), MediatR handlers for all operations, and controllers for the API surface. They integrate with other modules through events — a facility booking might trigger a payment, and equipment maintenance might trigger a communication to the groundskeeper.
