# Payments and Bookkeeping

Money is what keeps clubs running, and the Payments module is responsible for tracking every financial transaction that flows through the platform. This is not just a simple payment gateway integration — it is a comprehensive financial record-keeping system with support for multiple payment methods, invoicing, refunds, payment plans, transaction fee tracking, and principles borrowed from double-entry bookkeeping. Getting money wrong destroys trust faster than anything else, so this module is built with correctness as the primary concern.

A Payment entity represents a single financial transaction. It captures who paid (member ID), how much (amount as a decimal), how they paid (payment method — Stripe, PayPal, GoCardless, Cash, BankTransfer), what they paid for (payment type — Membership, SessionFee, EventTicket, ShopPurchase, Donation, Refund), when they paid (payment date), and the current status of the payment. The description field provides human-readable context like "Membership: Adult Full Member" or "Session attendance fee" or "Event ticket purchase."

The payment status lifecycle is enforced by the domain entity with the same state machine pattern used throughout the system. A payment starts as Pending — the payment has been initiated but not yet confirmed. From Pending, it can transition to Completed (payment was successfully processed), Failed (payment was declined or errored), or Cancelled (payment was abandoned before processing). From Completed, it can transition to Refunded (the payment was reversed). You cannot refund a failed payment. You cannot complete an already-completed payment. You cannot fail a completed payment. These transitions are enforced by the Complete, Fail, and Refund methods on the entity, each of which validates the current state before making the change.

When a payment completes, two additional pieces of information are recorded: the external transaction reference and the processing fee. The transaction reference is the identifier from the payment provider — a Stripe charge ID like "ch_1234567890" or a PayPal transaction ID. This allows reconciliation between our records and the payment provider's records. The processing fee is what the payment provider charged for processing the transaction — typically around 2.9 percent plus a fixed fee for card payments. Recording this separately from the payment amount allows accurate profit and loss reporting.

The platform's business model adds another layer of fees. When a payment is processed through the platform (as opposed to cash collected directly by the club), the platform takes a percentage — typically 1.5 to 2 percent on top of the payment provider's fee. So for a one-hundred-pound membership payment processed through Stripe, the breakdown might be: member pays one hundred pounds, Stripe takes two pounds ninety (2.9 percent), the platform takes one pound fifty (1.5 percent), and the club receives ninety-five pounds sixty. These platform fees are how The League generates revenue as a SaaS business, and they are tracked on each payment entity.

Invoicing is the flip side of payments — it represents money owed rather than money received. An Invoice entity has an invoice number (formatted like INV-TEDD-00042), an amount, a status (Draft, Sent, Paid, Overdue, Cancelled), an issued date, a due date, and a description of what the invoice is for. Invoices are typically generated automatically — when a membership renewal is due, the system creates an invoice. When a session fee is incurred, an invoice is created. The club manager can also create manual invoices for ad-hoc charges.

The invoice lifecycle connects to payments. When a member pays an invoice (either through the online payment flow or by the club recording a cash payment), the invoice status transitions from Sent to Paid and a corresponding Payment entity is created. If the due date passes without payment, the invoice moves to Overdue status, which can trigger automated reminder communications and potentially affect the member's standing with the club.

Member balances provide an aggregated financial view for each member. The MemberBalance entity tracks the total outstanding amount (what they owe across all unpaid invoices) and any credit amount (overpayments or refunds that have not been applied to future charges). The correctness property here is fundamental: a member's outstanding balance must always equal the sum of their unpaid invoice amounts, and their credit must always equal the sum of unapplied credits. If these numbers ever diverge from the underlying transaction records, something has gone wrong.

Payment plans support members who cannot pay a large amount upfront. Instead of paying three hundred pounds for an annual membership in one go, a member might arrange to pay fifty pounds per month for six months. A payment plan splits a single charge into multiple scheduled payments, each with its own due date. The system tracks which installments have been paid and which are outstanding. If an installment is missed, it follows the same dunning process as a failed subscription payment.

The multiple payment method support is implemented through a provider abstraction. The IPaymentProvider interface defines methods for creating charges, processing refunds, and checking payment status. In production, there would be concrete implementations for Stripe (card payments), GoCardless (direct debit), and PayPal. In development, a mock provider simulates successful and failed payments without requiring real payment credentials. The provider is selected based on the payment method specified in the request.

Stripe Connect is the production payment architecture. Rather than the platform collecting payments and then disbursing to clubs (which would require the platform to hold funds and deal with money transmission regulations), Stripe Connect allows the platform to act as a facilitator. Each club has a Stripe Connected Account. When a member pays, the money goes directly to the club's connected account, with the platform's fee automatically deducted. This is cleaner legally and operationally — the platform never holds club funds.

Refund processing reverses a completed payment. The refund amount can be full or partial. A full refund returns the entire payment amount to the member. A partial refund returns a portion — useful when a member attended half of a course before withdrawing. The refund creates a new Payment entity with type Refund and a negative amount (or a positive amount with a Refund type, depending on how you model it). The original payment's status changes to Refunded, and the member's balance is updated accordingly.

The seed data generates two hundred and fifty-one payments across all clubs and members over a twelve-month period. These include membership payments (one per member at their membership type's price), session fees (two to five per active member spread across the year), and event ticket purchases (zero to two per member). About ninety-five percent are completed successfully and five percent are failed, simulating declined cards. Payment dates are distributed across the last twelve months to give you realistic historical data for revenue charts, monthly income reports, and cash flow analysis.

The bookkeeping aspect of this module follows double-entry principles at a simplified level. Every financial event creates balanced records — a payment received creates a credit to the club's revenue and a debit to the member's balance. A refund creates the opposite entries. This ensures that the books always balance and that you can produce accurate financial statements at any point in time. The audit trail records every financial mutation with before and after values, timestamps, and the user who performed the action.
