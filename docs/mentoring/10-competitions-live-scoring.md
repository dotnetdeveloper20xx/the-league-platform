# Competitions and Live Scoring

Competitions are where sports clubs come alive. Leagues, cups, knockouts, championships — this is what members actually care about most passionately. The thrill of match day, the tension of a close league table, the drama of a cup semi-final. The Competitions module handles the full lifecycle from setting up a season to generating fixtures, recording match results, calculating standings, and broadcasting live scores through SignalR to spectators watching from home or the sideline.

The data model has a clear hierarchy that mirrors how competitions work in the real world. At the top is a Season entity. A season represents a time period during which competitions take place — "2024/25 Season" for a football club that plays August to May, or "2024 Season" for a cricket club that plays April to September. A season has a name, a start date, an end date, and an active flag. Most clubs have one active season at a time, though there might be overlap during transition periods.

Within a season, you have one or more Competition entities. A competition has a type — League, Cup, Championship, or Knockout — which determines how fixtures are generated and how standings are calculated. It has a name like "Saturday League Division 2" or "Inter-Club Championship." And critically, it has point values for wins, draws, and losses. A standard football league uses three points for a win and one for a draw. A cricket league might use twelve points for a win and four for a draw. Some competitions award bonus points for certain achievements. The point values are configurable per competition because different sports and different leagues have different scoring systems.

Within a competition, you have CompetitionTeam entities. These represent the teams participating in the competition. Importantly, these are not necessarily all teams from the same club. A club might run an internal league where their first eleven plays their second eleven, but they might also participate in an external league where they play against other local clubs. Each team has a name, a colour for display purposes (used in the UI for team badges and jersey indicators), and a squad size. The seed data creates four teams per competition, mixing the club's own teams with fictional opponents like "Hampton Hill CC" or "Surbiton HC."

Fixtures are matches between two teams. The Match entity is the core of the competition system. It records the home team ID, away team ID, match date, venue, match day number (which round of the competition this is), and the match status. The status lifecycle for a match is Scheduled (fixture is set but has not started), InProgress (match is currently being played), Completed (match is finished with a final result), Postponed (delayed to a future date), and Cancelled (will not be played).

For completed and in-progress matches, the entity stores the home score and away score as integers. The result is derived from the scores — HomeWin if home score is greater, AwayWin if away score is greater, Draw if equal, and NotPlayed for matches that have not yet produced a result. The result is stored explicitly rather than calculated on the fly because it simplifies standings calculations and historical queries.

Standings are the league table — the thing every sports fan checks obsessively. The Standing entity (or what we call StandingSeedDto in the seed data) tracks each team's record within a competition: played, won, drawn, lost, goals for (or runs for, or points for depending on the sport), goals against, goal difference, and total points. The points calculation is one of our most important correctness properties: for any team in any competition, their total points must always equal (wins multiplied by points-for-win) plus (draws multiplied by points-for-draw) plus (losses multiplied by points-for-loss). This is a mathematical invariant that must hold at all times.

Standings are recalculated whenever a match result is recorded or updated. The calculation iterates through all completed matches in the competition, tallies each team's results, and produces the standings table sorted by points descending, then goal difference, then goals scored. Position numbers are assigned based on this sorted order. Ties in points are broken by goal difference, then by goals scored — the standard tiebreaker rules used in most leagues.

Round-robin fixture generation is another correctness property. For a league competition with N teams, the system must generate exactly N multiplied by (N minus one) divided by two unique matches, ensuring every team plays every other team exactly once (for a single round-robin) or twice (for a double round-robin, home and away). The algorithm must produce a valid schedule where no team plays twice on the same match day. This is a well-known combinatorial problem with established algorithms, and our implementation must produce correct results for any number of teams.

Live scoring is where the real-time experience comes in, and this is powered by SignalR through the MatchCentreHub. When a match is in progress, the club manager or scorer updates the score through the API — posting a goal, recording a wicket, updating the over count. The API handler updates the Match entity and then broadcasts the update through the SignalR hub to all connected clients who are watching that match.

The frontend has a dedicated Match Centre page that connects to the MatchCentreHub when opened. It subscribes to updates for a specific match and renders a live scoreboard that updates in real time without page refreshes. When a goal is scored, the score changes on screen within seconds for everyone watching. This creates an engaging experience for members who cannot be at the ground — they can follow their club's matches live from their phone.

The scoring format is sport-aware, which adds complexity but is essential for a multi-sport platform. Cricket scoring involves runs, wickets, overs, and extras — a cricket score might be "247 for 6 in 42.3 overs." Football scoring is simply goals — "3-1." Hockey is also goals but with quarters rather than halves. The Competition entity's type and the club's sport configuration determine how scores are displayed, validated, and broadcast. A cricket match cannot have a score of "3-1" and a football match cannot have "247 for 6."

The seed data creates four seasons (one per club), four competitions (one per club), sixteen teams (four per competition), and twenty-four matches (six per competition). Of the six matches per competition, three are completed with random but realistic scores, one is marked as in progress (simulating a live match), and two are scheduled for the future. Standings are pre-calculated from the completed match results, giving you a working league table with correct point totals and positions. This data lets you immediately see the standings page, fixture list, and match detail views working with real numbers.

When extending this module, keep in mind that competitions generate data over an entire season. A league with ten teams playing each other twice produces ninety matches, each with potentially dozens of match events (goals, cards, substitutions). Query performance and pagination are important for historical views. The current season's data should be fast to access, while previous seasons can be loaded on demand.
