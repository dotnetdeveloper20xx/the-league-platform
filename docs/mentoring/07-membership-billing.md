# Membership and Billing

Memberships are how clubs make money, and the billing model needs to be flexible enough to handle the wild variety of ways sports clubs structure their fees. I have seen clubs that charge a flat annual fee to everyone. I have seen clubs with ten different membership categories based on age, playing status, and social access. I have seen clubs that charge monthly with direct debit, clubs that charge quarterly, and clubs that offer lifetime memberships for a single large payment. Our Memberships module handles all of these scenarios through a combination of membership types, billing cycles, and flexible pricing.

The foundation of the billing model is the MembershipType entity. Each club defines their own membership types — these are the products they sell to their members. A typical cricket club might have "Adult Full Member" at two hundred and eighty-five pounds per year (full playing and social rights), "Junior Member" at ninety-five pounds per year (for under-eighteens), "Social Member" at seventy-five pounds per year (clubhouse access but no playing rights), and "Family Membership" at five hundred and fifty pounds per year (covers two adults and up to three juniors). Each type is completely configurable by the club.

A MembershipType entity has several important properties. The name and description are what members see when choosing their membership. The price is the base cost. The billing cycle determines how often the member is charged — this is an enum with values like Annual, Monthly, Quarterly, SemiAnnual, Weekly, and Lifetime. The MinAge and MaxAge properties allow age-restricted memberships — a junior membership might require the member to be under eighteen, while an adult membership requires eighteen or over. The Capacity property sets an optional limit on how many members can hold this type simultaneously. The JoiningFee is a one-time charge on top of the first billing period's fee. And there are flags for whether the type is publicly visible and whether online signup is allowed.

The billing cycle drives several calculations throughout the system. When a membership is created, the end date is calculated from the start date based on the cycle — add one year for Annual, one month for Monthly, and so on. Lifetime memberships have no end date. When a membership approaches its end date, the system can trigger renewal reminders through the Communications module. When a member signs up mid-cycle (say, joining in October for an April-to-March annual membership), proration calculates the reduced fee for the remaining months.

Proration is one of our defined correctness properties. The calculation must be mathematically accurate to two decimal places. For an annual membership costing two hundred and forty pounds, joining six months into the year should result in a prorated fee of exactly one hundred and twenty pounds. Joining with three months remaining should be exactly sixty pounds. The formula is straightforward — (remaining days divided by total days in the cycle) multiplied by the full price, rounded to two decimal places — but getting it right in all edge cases (leap years, different month lengths, timezone boundaries) requires careful implementation.

A Membership entity represents the actual assignment of a member to a membership type. It is the record that says "Oliver Smith holds an Adult Full Member membership at Teddington Cricket Club, starting April first twenty twenty-four, ending March thirty-first twenty twenty-five, with auto-renewal enabled, and he paid two hundred and eighty-five pounds." The entity records the member ID, the membership type ID, the start date, end date, auto-renewal flag, the price actually paid (which might differ from the type's listed price due to discounts or proration), and the membership's status.

The membership status lifecycle is separate from the member status lifecycle, which is an important distinction. A member can be Active while their membership is Expired — this happens when someone's membership lapses but the club has not yet suspended or cancelled their member account. The membership statuses are Active (currently valid and paid), Expired (end date has passed without renewal), Frozen (temporarily paused), and Cancelled (terminated early). Each status transition is enforced by the domain entity with the same pattern as the Member state machine.

Freezing is a feature that many clubs need and few software systems handle well. If a member gets injured and cannot play for three months, the club can freeze their membership. During the freeze period, the member retains their membership but is not expected to pay (if on a monthly cycle) and the end date is extended by the freeze duration. So if someone has an annual membership ending in March and they freeze for three months starting in November, their new end date becomes June. The member does not lose time they have paid for. This is modelled as a method on the Membership entity that records the freeze start date and adjusts the end date accordingly.

Discounts add another layer of flexibility. A club might offer an early-bird discount for renewals before a certain date — renew by January thirty-first and get ten percent off. They might offer a family discount when multiple members from the same household sign up. They might offer a loyalty discount for members who have been with the club for more than five years. The discount amount or percentage is applied when the membership is created or renewed, and the actual price paid is stored on the Membership entity. This means the membership record is a complete financial record — you can always see what was actually charged, not just what the list price was.

Waitlists come into play when a membership type has a capacity limit. Some clubs genuinely have limited capacity — a golf club with only four hundred full memberships, or a cricket club that can only accommodate two hundred playing members on their fixture card. When all slots are taken, new applicants go on a waitlist. The waitlist is ordered by application date. When a slot opens up — through cancellation, expiry without renewal, or capacity increase — the next person on the waitlist gets notified and given a window to accept. If they do not accept within the window, the offer moves to the next person.

Auto-renewal is the default for most memberships. When a membership's end date approaches, the system attempts to charge the member's stored payment method for the renewal amount. If the payment succeeds, a new membership period begins seamlessly. If it fails, the system enters a dunning flow — retrying at configured intervals before eventually marking the membership as expired. The member receives notifications at each stage: upcoming renewal reminder, payment attempt, payment failure, final warning, and expiry notice.

The seed data creates fifteen membership types across four clubs with realistic pricing. Teddington has four types ranging from seventy-five to five hundred and fifty pounds. Highbury has three types including a monthly gym membership. Richmond has four types including a student discount tier. The MCC has four types including an eight-thousand-five-hundred-pound lifetime membership. Forty-three active memberships are created linking members to their types, with a mix of active and expired statuses and realistic start and end dates based on each member's join date.

When you are working on this module, the key insight is that MembershipType is the template and Membership is the instance. Types define what is available. Memberships record what was actually purchased. The type's price might change over time, but existing memberships retain the price that was paid. This separation is essential for accurate financial reporting and for honouring the terms members signed up under.
