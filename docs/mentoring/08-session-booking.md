# Session Booking

Sessions are the bread and butter of any sports club's weekly operations. Training sessions, coaching sessions, social games, match preparation, fitness classes — these are the recurring activities that members actually show up for week after week. Unlike events which are one-off occasions, sessions are the rhythm of club life. The Sessions module handles scheduling, booking, capacity management, waitlists, attendance tracking, cancellations, and fee collection. It is one of the most frequently used parts of the system from both the club manager and member perspectives.

A Session entity represents a single occurrence of an activity at a specific date and time. It has a title like "Senior Nets Practice" or "Youth Academy" or "5-a-side Social," a category that classifies it (Seniors, Juniors, Ladies, Mens, Mixed, Social, Advanced, AllAges), a start time as a full datetime, a duration in minutes, a capacity limit representing the maximum number of people who can attend, and an optional fee. Sessions belong to a club through the ClubId tenant discriminator, so each club only sees and manages their own sessions.

The category system deserves some explanation because it drives filtering and display logic on the frontend. When a member opens the session booking page, they typically want to see sessions relevant to them. A junior member wants to see Junior sessions. A women's team player wants to see Ladies sessions. The category allows the frontend to filter the session list without needing complex eligibility rules. AllAges and Social categories are shown to everyone. Sport-specific categories like Advanced might be shown only to members with certain membership types. The filtering logic lives in the frontend and the query handlers — the domain entity itself does not enforce who can book which category.

The booking flow is the core business process of this module. When a member wants to attend a session, they request a booking. The system checks the session's current capacity situation by comparing CurrentBookingCount against Capacity. If there is space available — CurrentBookingCount is less than Capacity — the booking is confirmed immediately. The member gets a confirmation, the CurrentBookingCount is incremented, and a booking record is created linking the member to the session with a Confirmed status.

If the session is full — CurrentBookingCount equals Capacity — the member has two options depending on the club's configuration. They can be placed on a waitlist, which creates a booking record with a Waitlisted status and a position number. Or they can be told the session is full and try a different one. The waitlist is ordered by request time — first come, first served.

Waitlist promotion is an important automated process. When someone cancels their confirmed booking for a session that has a waitlist, the system automatically promotes the next person on the waitlist to Confirmed status. Their booking status changes, the CurrentBookingCount stays the same (one person left, one person was promoted), and the promoted member receives a notification that they are now confirmed for the session. This happens without any manual intervention from the club manager.

Capacity management is a critical invariant in the system. The number of confirmed bookings must never exceed the session's capacity. This is one of our defined correctness properties — for all sessions at all times, confirmed booking count must be less than or equal to capacity. The CurrentBookingCount property on the Session entity tracks this, and the booking logic checks it atomically before confirming. In a concurrent environment where two members try to book the last slot simultaneously, the database's optimistic concurrency or the transaction isolation level prevents both from succeeding.

Attendance tracking happens after the session occurs. A coach or club manager opens the session's attendance view, which shows all confirmed bookings, and marks each member as Attended, NoShow, or Late. This is not just administrative record-keeping — it feeds into multiple other systems. The Analytics module uses attendance data to calculate member engagement scores. A member who books sessions but consistently does not show up has a declining engagement score, which might trigger a churn prediction alert. The Communications module can send automated messages to members who have missed multiple sessions without cancelling.

The attendance marking is done through the MarkAttendance endpoint, which accepts a list of attendance entries — each containing a member ID and their attendance status. This batch approach is more efficient than marking one member at a time, especially for sessions with twenty or thirty attendees. The handler validates that all specified members actually have confirmed bookings for the session before recording attendance.

Session cancellation has two flavours. A member can cancel their own booking, which frees up the slot and potentially promotes someone from the waitlist. The system might enforce a cancellation deadline — you cannot cancel within two hours of the session start time, for example. This prevents members from booking speculatively and cancelling at the last minute, which is frustrating for waitlisted members and makes capacity planning difficult for coaches.

The other flavour is club-level cancellation, where the club manager cancels the entire session. This might happen due to weather conditions (outdoor cricket nets cancelled due to rain), insufficient numbers (only two people booked for a session that needs at least eight), or facility unavailability (the pitch is waterlogged). When a session is cancelled, all bookings are automatically cancelled, all members are notified, and the IsCancelled flag on the session prevents any new bookings. If the session had a fee, refund processing would be triggered through the Payments module.

The fee model is intentionally simple. Some sessions are free — they are included as part of the membership. Weekly training for full members might have no additional charge. Other sessions have a per-attendance fee. A social five-a-side might cost eight pounds per person to cover pitch hire. A specialist coaching session might cost twenty-five pounds. When a session has a fee and a booking is confirmed, a payment record needs to be created. This cross-module communication happens through integration events — the Sessions module publishes a SessionBooked event containing the session ID, member ID, and fee amount, and the Payments module subscribes to create the corresponding payment record.

Recurring sessions are a concept that exists at the scheduling level rather than the entity level. A club does not create three hundred and sixty-five individual session records for "Tuesday Nets." Instead, they define a recurring schedule — every Tuesday at 6pm, duration two hours, capacity twenty-four, fee five pounds — and the system generates individual session instances from that schedule. Each generated instance is a standalone Session entity that can be individually modified (change the time for one week because of a bank holiday) or cancelled without affecting the others.

The seed data generates approximately three hundred and sixty-five sessions across four clubs over a six-month period. Each club has three session types that recur weekly on specific days. Teddington has Senior Nets on Tuesdays, Junior Coaching on Wednesdays, and Weekend Match Prep on Thursdays. Highbury has First Team Training on Tuesdays, Youth Academy on Saturdays, and 5-a-side Social on Fridays. Each session has a realistic booking count between forty and ninety-five percent of capacity, and about five percent are randomly cancelled. Future sessions are included so you can test the booking flow with sessions that have not happened yet.

The practical consideration when extending this module is that sessions generate a lot of data over time. A club with three sessions per week generates over one hundred and fifty sessions per year, each with potentially dozens of bookings and attendance records. Query performance matters here — the indexes on ClubId plus StartTime and ClubId plus Category are essential for the common queries of "show me upcoming sessions" and "show me all junior sessions this month." Pagination is important for historical views, and the frontend should default to showing only future sessions unless the user explicitly requests history.
