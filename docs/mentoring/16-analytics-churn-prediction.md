# Analytics and Churn Prediction

The Analytics module is where data becomes insight. Every other module in the system generates data — members join and leave, sessions are booked and attended, payments succeed and fail, events fill up or sit empty. The Analytics module takes all of that raw operational data and transforms it into actionable intelligence that helps club managers make better decisions. It calculates health scores, predicts which members are at risk of leaving, forecasts revenue, benchmarks clubs against anonymized platform averages, and surfaces the metrics that matter most on dashboards.

The centrepiece of the analytics system is the Club Health Score — a single number from zero to one hundred that represents the overall vitality of a club. Think of it like a credit score but for club operations. A score of eighty-five means the club is thriving — good member retention, healthy attendance, strong finances, active engagement. A score of forty-five means there are problems — members are leaving, sessions are empty, payments are failing, engagement is declining. The health score gives club managers an at-a-glance understanding of how their club is doing without needing to dig into individual metrics.

The health score is calculated from weighted components. Member retention contributes a significant portion — what percentage of members renewed their membership this year compared to last year? A retention rate of ninety percent is excellent; sixty percent is concerning. Session attendance contributes another portion — what is the average attendance rate across all sessions? If sessions are consistently half-empty, something is wrong with the scheduling, pricing, or communication. Financial health looks at payment success rates, outstanding balances, and revenue trends. Engagement measures how actively members are using the platform — booking sessions, registering for events, updating their profiles.

Each component is scored independently on a zero-to-one-hundred scale, then combined using configurable weights. The default weights might be thirty percent retention, twenty-five percent attendance, twenty-five percent financial health, and twenty percent engagement. But clubs can adjust these weights based on what matters most to them. A club that is primarily social might weight engagement higher. A club with expensive facilities might weight financial health higher.

The ClubAnalyticsSnapshot entity captures the health score and its components at a point in time. Snapshots are generated periodically — daily or weekly — creating a time series that shows how the club's health is trending. Is the score improving month over month? Is it declining? Are there seasonal patterns (cricket clubs naturally have lower engagement in winter)? The historical snapshots enable trend analysis and help clubs understand whether their interventions are working.

Churn prediction is perhaps the most valuable analytical capability in the system. Churn means a member leaving the club — not renewing their membership, cancelling, or simply disappearing. Losing a member is expensive. The club loses their membership fee, their session fees, their event attendance, and their social contribution to the club community. Acquiring a new member to replace them costs marketing effort and time. If you can identify members who are likely to churn before they actually leave, you can intervene — reach out personally, offer a discount, invite them to a social event, address whatever is causing their disengagement.

The ChurnPrediction entity represents a risk assessment for a specific member. It includes a risk score from zero to one hundred (higher means more likely to churn), the factors contributing to the risk, and a recommended intervention. The prediction is generated by analyzing multiple signals from the member's behaviour over time.

The signals that indicate churn risk are well-established in subscription businesses and apply equally to sports clubs. Declining attendance is the strongest signal — if a member who used to attend two sessions per week has not attended any in the last month, they are disengaging. Missed payments are another strong signal — a member who lets an invoice go overdue might be questioning whether the membership is worth the cost. Reduced platform engagement — not logging in, not booking sessions, not responding to communications — suggests the club is no longer top of mind for them.

The churn prediction algorithm combines these signals with configurable thresholds. A member gets risk points for each signal: twenty points for no attendance in thirty days, fifteen points for a missed payment, ten points for no login in fourteen days, ten points for declining attendance trend (attending less frequently than their historical average), and so on. The total risk score determines the risk category: Low (zero to thirty), Medium (thirty to sixty), High (sixty to eighty), and Critical (eighty to one hundred).

For members in the High and Critical categories, the system generates recommended interventions. These might be "Send a personal check-in email from the club manager," "Offer a session credit to encourage attendance," "Invite to the upcoming social event," or "Schedule a phone call to discuss their experience." The recommendations are based on which risk factors are most prominent — if the issue is attendance, the intervention focuses on getting them back to sessions. If the issue is financial, the intervention might involve a payment plan or temporary fee reduction.

The MemberEngagement entity tracks engagement metrics for each member over time. It records their last login date, their session attendance rate (sessions attended divided by sessions booked), their event participation rate, their communication response rate (do they open emails?), and an overall engagement score. This data feeds into both the churn prediction and the club health score.

Revenue forecasting uses historical payment data to project future income. The simplest model looks at recurring revenue — how many active memberships are there, what are their values, and when do they renew? This gives a baseline forecast. More sophisticated models factor in historical churn rates (some members will not renew), seasonal patterns (new member signups peak in spring for cricket clubs and autumn for football clubs), and growth trends. The forecast is presented as a range — optimistic, expected, and pessimistic scenarios — giving club treasurers a realistic picture for budgeting.

Platform benchmarking compares a club's metrics against anonymized averages across all clubs on the platform. A club manager can see that their member retention rate of seventy-eight percent is below the platform average of eighty-three percent, which tells them there is room for improvement. Or they can see that their session attendance rate of seventy-two percent is above average, which validates their scheduling approach. Benchmarking provides context — a number in isolation is meaningless, but a number compared to peers is actionable.

The benchmarking data is anonymized — no club can see another specific club's data. The platform calculates averages, medians, and percentiles across all clubs (or clubs of the same sport, or clubs of similar size) and presents these as reference points. This is a platform-level feature that requires aggregating data across tenants, which is one of the few operations that intentionally bypasses the tenant query filter.

The Analytics module integrates with the dashboard views on the frontend. The Club Manager dashboard shows the health score prominently, along with key metrics like active member count, this month's revenue, upcoming session attendance, and any critical churn alerts. The SuperAdmin dashboard shows platform-wide metrics — total clubs, total members, platform revenue, and clubs that might need attention (low health scores or failed subscription payments).

The data pipeline for analytics is designed to be non-blocking. Calculating health scores and churn predictions involves querying across multiple modules' data — member records, session attendance, payment history, login activity. These calculations should not slow down the operational system. In production, they would run as background jobs (using Hangfire) during off-peak hours, storing the results in the analytics schema for fast retrieval. The dashboard reads pre-calculated snapshots rather than computing metrics on the fly.

The seed data does not pre-populate analytics snapshots because they are derived from operational data that is generated by the seeder. Once the seeder has run and created members, sessions, payments, and attendance records, the analytics calculations can be triggered to generate the initial snapshots. This is a deliberate sequencing — analytics depends on operational data existing first.

When extending this module, keep in mind that analytics is read-heavy and write-light. The calculations happen periodically in the background, and the results are read frequently by dashboards. Optimize for read performance — denormalize where it makes sense, pre-calculate aggregates, and cache results that do not change frequently. The health score does not need to be recalculated on every page load — once per day is sufficient for most clubs.
