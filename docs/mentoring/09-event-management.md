# Event Management

Events are different from sessions in an important way that justifies them being a separate module entirely. Sessions are recurring, routine activities — the weekly training that happens every Tuesday at the same time in the same place. Events are one-off occasions — the annual awards dinner, a charity match, a quiz night fundraiser, the AGM, a coaching masterclass with a guest speaker. They have a richer lifecycle, they can be ticketed with different pricing tiers, they support RSVP for free events, they have marketing and promotion needs, and they often involve logistics that sessions do not — catering, venue hire, guest speakers, sponsors.

An Event entity has considerably more metadata than a Session. It has a title, a description for marketing purposes, an event type (Social, AGM, Tournament, Fundraiser, Presentation, Training), a start datetime and end datetime (events have explicit durations rather than a start time plus minutes), a venue name, a capacity, and detailed pricing information. Events can be ticketed — meaning attendees pay for a ticket — or free with RSVP. Ticketed events have both a standard price and a member price, because clubs typically offer discounts to their own members compared to guests or the general public.

The lifecycle of an event is modelled as a state machine with seven states, which is more complex than sessions because events have a longer planning and promotion cycle. An event starts in Draft status — the club manager is still setting up the details, writing the description, confirming the venue, setting the price. Nothing is visible to members at this stage. When the manager is happy with everything, they publish the event, which moves it to Published status. This makes it visible on the club's event calendar and in member communications, but registration is not yet open.

Opening registration is a separate deliberate action that moves the event to RegistrationOpen status. This separation between Published and RegistrationOpen exists because clubs often want to announce an event and build anticipation before actually allowing signups. They might publish the event two months in advance but only open registration one month before. During the RegistrationOpen phase, members can register, buy tickets, or RSVP.

Registration can be closed in two ways. The club manager can manually close it by transitioning to RegistrationClosed — perhaps because they need final numbers for catering a week before the event. Or it closes automatically when capacity is reached. Once registration is closed, no new registrations are accepted but existing ones remain valid.

On the day of the event, the status moves to InProgress. After the event concludes, it moves to Completed. At any point before completion, the event can be Cancelled, which is a terminal state that triggers notifications to all registered attendees and potentially initiates refund processing.

Each state transition is enforced by the domain entity through methods like Publish, OpenRegistration, CloseRegistration, Start, Complete, and Cancel. You cannot open registration on a draft event — it must be published first. You cannot complete an event that has not started. You cannot start an event whose registration is still open (you must close registration first, or it closes automatically when the event starts). These rules prevent invalid states and ensure the event follows a logical progression that makes sense to both administrators and attendees.

Registration is handled through the EventRegistration entity, which is the equivalent of a booking for events. When a member registers for a ticketed event, the system creates a registration record with a unique ticket number and a QR code. The ticket number follows a format like TKT-TEDD-000042, incorporating the club identifier for easy visual identification. The QR code encodes the registration ID and ticket number in a format that can be scanned at the door.

For non-ticketed events (free events with RSVP), the registration still creates a record but without ticket numbers or QR codes. The registration tracks the member ID, the event ID, the registration timestamp, whether it is confirmed, and a guest count. The guest count allows members to indicate they are bringing additional people — "I am coming and bringing two guests" — which helps the club plan for capacity and catering.

The check-in flow on the day of the event uses the QR code or ticket number. A club manager or volunteer at the door has the check-in interface open on their phone or tablet. They scan the QR code (or manually enter the ticket number), and the system looks up the registration, verifies it is valid and has not already been checked in, and marks it as attended. The interface shows a green confirmation with the member's name so the door person can greet them. If the ticket is invalid or already used, it shows a red rejection. This is a simple but effective way to manage event attendance without paper lists or manual tick-sheets.

Pricing flexibility is important for events. The standard price is what non-members or the general public pay. The member price is the discounted rate for club members. Some events might be free for members but charged for guests. Some might have early-bird pricing that changes after a certain date. The current implementation supports standard and member pricing, with the actual price paid recorded on the registration entity. More complex pricing (early-bird, group discounts, VIP tiers) could be added by extending the pricing model without changing the core registration flow.

Event cancellation is a significant operation because it affects everyone who has registered. When a club manager cancels an event, the system needs to notify all registered attendees, process refunds for ticketed events, and update the event status. The cancellation reason is recorded for transparency. Partial cancellations — like changing the date rather than cancelling entirely — are handled by cancelling the original event and creating a new one, with communications sent to registered members offering them automatic transfer to the new date.

The integration with other modules is important to understand. Events integrate with Communications for sending registration confirmations, event reminders (one week before, one day before), and cancellation notices. They integrate with Payments for processing ticket purchases and refunds. They integrate with Analytics for tracking which types of events get the best attendance and which members are most engaged with the social side of the club.

The seed data creates fourteen events across four clubs — a mix of social events, AGMs, tournaments, fundraisers, presentations, and training events. They are spread across the next two months with various statuses: some in draft (still being planned), some published (announced but registration not yet open), and some with registration open (actively accepting signups). Prices range from ten pounds for a quiz night to ninety-five pounds for a heritage tour and dinner at Lord's. This gives you realistic data for building event listing pages, registration flows, the check-in interface, and event management dashboards.

The design decision to keep Events and Sessions as separate modules despite some surface similarity is worth understanding. Their lifecycles are fundamentally different — sessions are simple and recurring, events are complex and one-off. Their booking models differ — sessions have simple capacity checks, events have ticketing and RSVP. Their business rules differ — sessions have attendance marking, events have check-in with QR codes. Keeping them separate means each module stays focused and does not accumulate conditional logic to handle both cases. If you find yourself thinking "this is similar to how sessions work," that is fine — similar patterns can exist in different modules without needing to be unified into a shared abstraction.
